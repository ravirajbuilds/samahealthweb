# Case Studies & Testimonials

How preventive screening plays out when it actually reaches people — at the antenatal
visit, the community camp, the school health day, and the follow-up that usually never
happens. These are composites drawn from routine work at **Anubhav Life Care** (our
NABL-accredited hub in Barasat) and the camps and spokes around **North 24 Parganas**.

## What the device actually is (so the cases read correctly)

- **SamaClip** — a painless, **four-wavelength**, non-invasive fingertip screening clip. It
  is the device behind every *screening* result in these case studies: a haemoglobin /
  anaemia estimate, SpO₂, a heart-rate and rhythm signal (including an atrial-fibrillation
  flag), and an HbA1c **proxy** for diabetes risk. It produces a **screening signal, not a
  diagnosis** — every flag is confirmed against a laboratory or clinician before treatment.
- **SamaBeat (SamaBand)** — a low-power wristband for **follow-up monitoring** after a flag
  has been raised and confirmed. It supports continuity of care; it makes **no diagnostic
  accuracy claim**.
- **Tuberculosis** appears only as a **triage / referral signal** — a prompt to send someone
  for proper TB testing, never a TB diagnosis.

## Case Study 1 — The antenatal flag that changed a delivery plan

**Who:** "Rumana," 24, second pregnancy, a village in the Deganga block of North 24 Parganas.
**When:** Antenatal community camp, September 2025.
**Context:** West Bengal has the highest anaemia prevalence among women of reproductive age
of any Indian state (NFHS-5: 71.4%). Most of it is found late, or not at all.

**The screening.** Rumana came to a camp for a routine antenatal check, not because she felt
unwell — she put her tiredness down to the pregnancy and the heat. A SamaClip screen took
under two minutes and estimated her haemoglobin in the **moderate-anaemia** range. Because
SamaClip is a screen and not a diagnosis, the camp operator flagged her for a confirmatory
venous haemoglobin at the Anubhav Life Care lab the same week.

**What happened.** The lab confirmed iron-deficiency anaemia. She was started on iron
supplementation, given dietary counselling, and — critically — **enrolled in follow-up**.
A SamaBeat band and a simple re-screen schedule meant her haemoglobin trend was watched
across the third trimester instead of guessed at.

**Outcome.** Her haemoglobin recovered into a safer range before delivery, and her delivery
plan was adjusted with the anaemia on record rather than as a surprise on the day. The point
of the case is not a miracle; it is that a common, fixable condition was found early, in a
woman who would not otherwise have been tested.

## Case Study 2 — An asymptomatic atrial-fibrillation flag in a 61-year-old

**Who:** "Subir Babu," 61, retired schoolteacher, Madhyamgram.
**When:** Hypertension follow-up camp, November 2025.
**Context:** Atrial fibrillation is often silent and is a major, preventable cause of stroke.
People rarely walk into a clinic *for* a rhythm they cannot feel.

**The screening.** Subir came in to have his blood pressure checked. During the same sitting,
the SamaClip rhythm channel raised an **atrial-fibrillation flag** (the flag carries an AUC of
0.94 in our validation, but it remains a *screening flag*). The operator did not tell him he
had AFib — they told him the screen had picked up something in his heart rhythm that a doctor
needed to look at, and arranged a referral.

**What happened.** A 12-lead ECG and a cardiologist review at the district level confirmed
atrial fibrillation. He was assessed for stroke risk and started on appropriate anticoagulation
and rate control under specialist care.

**Outcome.** A silent, stroke-causing rhythm was caught and managed before it caused harm —
during a visit he had booked for something else entirely. This is the whole argument for
opportunistic screening: the highest-value finds are the ones the patient didn't come in for.

> *Related reading: "Atrial Fibrillation: The Silent Heart Rhythm That Raises Stroke Risk,"
> "Hypertension and Heart Disease: India's Quiet Epidemic," "What '120-Second Screening'
> Actually Looks Like."*

## Case Study 3 — A school screening day and the adolescent-anaemia iceberg

**Who:** A girls' secondary school, Habra block. ~180 students screened over two days.
**When:** Adolescent health drive, October 2025.
**Context:** Anaemia in adolescent girls sets a lower baseline for the rest of adult life and
compounds into the next generation. It is almost never screened for at this age.

**The screening.** Operators trained through our **train-the-trainer** programme ran SamaClip
screens during school hours — painless, no needle, no fasting, ~2 minutes each. A meaningful
share of students screened in the low-haemoglobin range **[VERIFY: exact prevalence for this
cohort]**, consistent with what state data would predict.

**What happened.** Flagged students were referred for confirmatory testing through the
hub-and-spoke link to Anubhav Life Care, with parental consent. Those confirmed were started
on iron supplementation and a re-screen was scheduled for the same school term, so the
intervention could be *checked*, not just prescribed.

**Outcome.** A condition that normally stays invisible until it shows up in a future pregnancy
was made visible and actionable at the point where it is cheapest to fix. The school now treats
screening as a recurring fixture, not a one-off event.

> *Related reading: "Anaemia and Adolescent Girls: Catching It Before It Compounds,"
> "Train-the-Trainer: How Local People Run Their Own Screening," "The Hub-and-Spoke Model."*

## Case Study 4 — Diabetes risk in a woman who had never reached a lab

**Who:** "Anjali," 46, runs a small tailoring business, Barasat town.
**When:** Women's health check-up camp, August 2025.
**Context:** Many women never reach a diagnostic lab until something is already wrong. A
non-invasive risk signal can change who gets tested at all.

**The screening.** At a five-in-one women's check-up (haemoglobin, SpO₂, heart rate, rhythm,
and a diabetes-risk signal), SamaClip's **HbA1c proxy** (validation r = 0.87) put Anjali in an
elevated-risk band. Again: a *risk signal*, not a diagnosis.

**What happened.** She was referred for a confirmatory HbA1c blood test at the hub, which
confirmed she was in the pre-diabetes range. She received lifestyle counselling and was put on
a monitoring and re-screen schedule rather than being lost back into the crowd.

**Outcome.** Pre-diabetes caught at the "still reversible with lifestyle" stage instead of
arriving years later as a complication. The non-invasive screen did the one thing a distant,
needle-based lab test had never managed to do for her: it actually happened.

> *Related reading: "Diabetes Risk in Women Who Never Reach a Lab," "Diabetes Is Rising in
> Semi-Urban Bengal. Here's What HbA1c Tells You," "Women's Health Check-Ups: Five Things
> Worth Screening in One Visit."*

## Case Study 5 — A TB triage flag, handled honestly

**Who:** "Mofizul," 38, agricultural labourer, Swarupnagar block.
**When:** General screening camp, December 2025.
**Context:** TB is endemic and frequently caught late. SamaHealth does **not** diagnose TB.

**The screening.** Mofizul came in with a haemoglobin concern. During screening, the workflow
raised a **TB triage signal** (persistent symptoms + risk pattern) — explicitly a prompt to
refer, not a result. The operator referred him into the public TB programme for sputum testing.

**Outcome.** He entered the proper diagnostic pathway weeks earlier than he otherwise would
have. The case is included precisely because it shows the boundary we hold: a triage signal
routes a person to the right test; it never pretends to *be* that test.

> *Related reading: "Tuberculosis Triage: How Screening Flags Who Needs Referral,"
> "What 'Screening, Not Diagnosis' Means — and Why the Distinction Matters."*

## Case Study 6 — Scaling the model across North 24 Parganas (operational)

**The challenge.** North 24 Parganas is home to roughly ten million people, with a wide gap
between where diagnostics exist and where people live.

**The model.** A trusted **hub** (Anubhav Life Care, NABL-accredited) anchors a set of
**spokes**: community camps, antenatal visits, schools, and follow-up clinics, each run by
**locally trained operators** rather than fly-in staff. Painless SamaClip screening removes the
finger-prick barrier; **free transport for pregnant and elderly patients** closes the last mile
between a flag and confirmatory care; SamaBeat carries the follow-up.

**The economics.** At roughly **$1.50 per non-invasive screen**, opportunistic screening is a
rounding error next to the cost of a late-stage cardiac, diabetic, or maternal crisis — which
is the comparison that actually matters.

**Where it stands.** Routine SamaClip screening has been running at the hub since **June 2025**;
the **validation study (n = 175)** ran Oct–Dec 2025 and results were published in **January
2026**; **SamaBeat** follow-up monitoring was added in the same window. Site counts, total
people screened to date, and per-camp volumes are tracked internally **[VERIFY]** and reported
honestly (we separate *footfall* from *people screened* on purpose).

> Preventive Screening: $1.50 vs the Cost of a Crisis," "How We Count Impact Honestly:
> Footfall Versus People Screened," "Free Transport for Pregnant and Elderly Patients."*

## Methodology & honest claims

These accounts are written to be *usable as marketing without becoming misleading*. The rules
we hold to:

1. **All accuracy figures belong to SamaClip and to the n = 175 validation study** (Oct–Dec
   2025, published Jan 2026): haemoglobin r = 0.91; anaemia AUC 0.92; SpO₂ bias −0.31% across
   the Monk Skin Tone scale; HbA1c-proxy r = 0.87; atrial-fibrillation flag AUC 0.94. SamaClip
   is **four-wavelength**.
2. **SamaBeat / SamaBand is follow-up monitoring only** — no diagnostic accuracy is claimed for it.
3. **Tuberculosis is a triage/referral signal**, never a diagnosis.
4. **Screening is not diagnosis.** Every flag in every case study above is confirmed by a
   laboratory test or a clinician before any treatment decision.
5. **Composite & consented.** Stories are representative composites; identifying details are
   changed; quotes are illustrative of consented real experiences.
6. **[VERIFY] = do not publish as fact yet** — founding-year and team-size specifics, exact site
   counts, daily footfall, per-cohort prevalence, and any named partnership (incl. Graywards)
   need internal sign-off before they go public.
